Trotterization and quantum simulation

Historical note

In 1982, Richard Feynman  asked whether quantum systems could be efficiently simulated on classical computers, and conjectured that the answer was no—but that a quantum computer could do the job. Seth Lloyd  proved Feynman right in 1996, showing that local Hamiltonians can be efficiently simulated on a quantum computer via the Lie-Trotter product formula. Quantum simulation remains one of the most promising near-term applications of quantum computers, with potential impact on quantum chemistry, materials science, and high-energy physics.

Why simulate quantum systems?

Complex quantum systems have numerous industrially significant applications, from molecular structure for quantum chemistry through to materials design. Despite their importance, progress on understanding the physics of such systems faces continuing challenges.

The direct simulation of quantum systems by classical computers is possible in principle but generically inefficient. Consider the Schrödinger equation for a many-body quantum system: $$\label{eq:schrodinger} \boxed{i\frac{d}{dt}|\psi\rangle = H |\psi\rangle.}$$ If one writes this equation in terms of a basis for Hilbert space, one obtains a system of coupled linear ODEs. Such systems are straightforward to solve numerically. So what is the difficulty?

Physical intuition

The key challenge is the exponential number of differential equations that must be solved. For one qubit, 2 equations; for two qubits, 4; and for n qubits, 2n equations. For a system of just 300 qubits, there are more equations than atoms in the observable universe (2300 ≈ 1090). Sometimes insightful approximations reduce the effective number of equations (e.g., tensor network methods for low-entanglement states), but for many physically interesting systems no such shortcuts are known. This is one of the strongest motivations for building quantum computers.

Many-body quantum systems

A many-body quantum system is comprised of many quantum degrees of freedom. We model our system as a collection of n qubits (assuming distinguishable particles for simplicity) with Hilbert space ℋ ≡ (ℂ2)⊗n.

Definition.

A Hamiltonian H acting on n qubits is called c-local if it can be written as $$H = \sum_{j=1}^L h_j,$$ where each operator hj acts nontrivially on at most c qubits (for some constant c independent of n). The number L of terms can be any polynomial in n.

Example.

The transverse-field Ising model on a chain of n qubits is $$H = \sum_{j=1}^{n-1} \paulix_j\paulix_{j+1} + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^n \pauliz_j,$$ where λ ∈ ℝ is the transverse field strength. This is a 2-local Hamiltonian with L = 2n − 1 terms. It models magnetic phase transitions and is one of the simplest exactly-solvable interacting quantum systems.

Remark.

The restriction to constant locality c implies that L is bounded by a polynomial in n. Indeed, there are at most $\binom{n}{c} = O\!\left(n^c\right)$ subsets of size c, so $L \le \binom{n}{c}$, which is polynomial for fixed c.

The quantum simulation algorithm

The goal of quantum simulation is, for a given time t, to prepare the state |ψ(t)⟩=e−iHt|ψ(0)⟩, corresponding to the solution of the Schrödinger equation [eq:schrodinger]. The objective is to implement the unitary U(t) ≡ e−iHt with quantum gates.

If we naïvely applied the universality theorem from Section [sec:circuits], we would need roughly 22n elementary gates—and even computing a single matrix element of U(t) requires solving the Schrödinger equation in the first place. We need a fundamentally different strategy.

Physical intuition

Although e−iHt is hard to implement directly, each factor e−ihjt acts on a small subsystem (at most c qubits) and is therefore straightforward to compile into a quantum circuit using O (1) gates. The challenge is that in general [hj, hk] ≠ 0, so $e^{-iHt} \neq \prod_{j=1}^L e^{-ih_j t}$. The Lie-Trotter formula provides a systematic way to overcome this obstacle.

Proposition.

If [hj, hk] = 0 for all j, k, then $$U(t) = e^{-iHt} = \prod_{j=1}^L e^{-ih_jt}$$ for all t.

Proof. When all terms commute, the exponential of a sum equals the product of exponentials. This follows from the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff formula, which states $e^A e^B = e^{A+B+\frac{1}{2}[A,\,B]+\cdots}$; all correction terms vanish when [A, B] = 0. ◻

The Lie-Trotter formula

Theorem.

Let A and B be Hermitian operators. Then for any real t, $$\boxed{\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \left(e^{iA\frac{t}{n}}e^{iB\frac{t}{n}}\right)^{n} = e^{i(A+B)t}.}$$

Proof. By the Taylor theorem with remainder, $$e^{iA\frac{t}{n}} = \mathbb{I} + \frac{1}{n} iAt + O\!\left(\frac{|t|^2\lVert A \rVert^2}{n^2}\right),$$ so that $$e^{iA\frac{t}{n}}e^{iB\frac{t}{n}} = \mathbb{I} + \frac{1}{n} i(A+B)t + O\!\left(\frac{|t|^2(\lVert A \rVert+\lVert B \rVert)^2}{n^2}\right).$$ Taking the nth power, using $\frac{1}{n^k}\binom{n}{k} = \frac{1}{k!}\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right)$, we obtain $$\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \left(e^{iA\frac{t}{n}}e^{iB\frac{t}{n}}\right)^{n} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(it)^k}{k!}(A+B)^k = e^{i(A+B)t}. \qedhere$$ ◻

By inductively applying the Lie-Trotter formula, one demonstrates that for n large enough, $$U(t) = e^{-it\sum_{j=1}^L h_j} \approx \left(e^{-i h_1\frac{t}{n}}e^{-i h_2\frac{t}{n}} \cdots e^{-i h_L\frac{t}{n}}\right)^{n}.$$ Notice that this is now a product of local unitary operators, each computable with O (1) gates.

Key result

The first-order Trotter approximation satisfies $$\lVert e^{-iHt} - \left(\prod_{j=1}^L e^{-ih_j t/n}\right)^n \rVert \le \frac{t^2}{2n}\sum_{j<k}\lVert [h_j,\,h_k] \rVert.$$ To achieve accuracy ϵ, one needs n = O (t2L2∥h∥2/ϵ) Trotter steps, giving a total gate count of O (t2L3∥h∥2/ϵ).

The quantum simulation algorithm

Algorithm

  • Input: (1) A Hamiltonian $H = \sum_{j=1}^L h_j$ acting on n qubits, where each hj acts on a constant-size subsystem; (2) an initial state |ψ0⟩; (3) a target accuracy δ > 0; and (4) a final time tf.

  • Output: A state |ψ̃(tf)⟩ such that |⟨ψ̃(tf)|e−iHtfψ0⟩|2 ≥ 1 − δ.

  • Runtime: $O\!\left(\poly{1/\delta}\right)$ operations.

  • Procedure: Define the Trotter step UΔt ≡ e−ih1Δte−ih2Δt⋯e−ihLΔt and choose Δt for acceptable error:

    1. Initialise: |ψ0⟩; set j = 0.

    2. Update: |ψ̃j + 1⟩=UΔt|ψ̃j⟩.

    3. Loop: j ← j + 1; repeat step 2 until j ⋅ Δt ≥ tf.

    4. Output: |ψ̃(tf)⟩=|ψ̃j⟩.

Physical intuition

Trotterization is analogous to the operator-splitting methods used in classical numerical analysis. By dividing the total evolution time into many small steps Δt, the error from non-commutativity is controlled: during each small step, the terms “almost commute” because their commutator is multiplied by Δt2. Higher-order product formulas

The first-order Trotter formula can be improved. The second-order Suzuki-Trotter formula uses S2(Δt) = e−ih1Δt/2e−ih2Δt/2⋯e−ihLΔt⋯e−ih2Δt/2e−ih1Δt/2, which achieves error O (Δt3) per step (compared to O (Δt2) for first order), giving a total error of O (t3/n2) after n steps. Even higher-order formulas can be constructed recursively .

Advanced techniques and further reading

With the deployment of near-term quantum devices, new possibilities for quantum simulation have emerged. Beyond Lie-Trotter expansions, the major modern approaches include:

  • Quantum signal processing and quantum singular value transform (QSVT) : A unifying framework that achieves near-optimal Hamiltonian simulation and encompasses many quantum algorithms as special cases.

  • Qubitization : A quantum walk-based method that achieves optimal scaling in all parameters simultaneously.

  • Linear combination of unitaries (LCU) : Expresses e−iHt as a linear combination of easily-implementable unitaries, using an ancilla register and amplitude amplification.

  • Randomised methods (qDRIFT) : Randomly samples terms from the Hamiltonian at each step, achieving gate complexity independent of the number of terms L.

Remark.

The optimal query complexity for Hamiltonian simulation is Θ(t∥H∥+log(1/ϵ)/loglog(1/ϵ)), achieved by QSVT-based methods. The Trotter approach, while not optimal, remains practically competitive due to its simplicity and favourable constant factors.
